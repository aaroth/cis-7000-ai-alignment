00 Overview

Alignment as a problem of collective decision-making

The course connects social choice, learning theory, game theory, information economics, and interactive decision-making. Rather than treating alignment as a single objective, we ask where objectives come from, what feedback can reveal, and how strategic environments shape the result.

I

Preferences

Representing, eliciting, and aggregating the values of many people.

II

Information

Turning predictions, comparisons, and conversation into good decisions.

III

Institutions

Designing incentives and interactions when models and providers are strategic.

01–05 Topics

A working map of the course

Five modules move from individual feedback to the institutions in which aligned systems must operate.

01

Preferences / Aggregation

From feedback to social objectives

Preference learning is also a problem of social choice: many judgments must become one training signal.

  • RLHF as social aggregation
  • Heterogeneous preferences and cycles
  • Arrow’s impossibility theorem
  • Cardinal utility and Harsanyi aggregation

02

Preferences / Learning

What preference optimization implements

Different learning rules encode different aggregation choices—even when they begin with the same comparisons.

  • Scalar reward fitting and DPO
  • Maximal lotteries and no-regret self-play
  • Regularized Nash learning
  • Welfare distortion and identifiability

03

Information / Decisions

Decision-aligned prediction

A prediction is useful when it supports downstream action, not merely when it scores well in isolation.

  • Calibration and decision regret
  • Omniprediction and indistinguishability
  • Blackwell approachability
  • Tractable human–model agreement

04

Information / Assistance

Verification, commitment, and assistance

Interactive protocols can expand oversight, but strategic communication changes what information is revealed.

  • AI debate and interactive verification
  • Bayesian persuasion
  • Deference and the off-switch game
  • Cooperative inverse reinforcement learning

05

Institutions / Competition

Institutions and competition

When no single system is perfectly aligned, competition and evaluation can sometimes recover aligned outcomes.

  • Best-AI selection
  • Emergent alignment through competition
  • Equilibrium welfare guarantees
  • Evaluation error and assumption audits

Optional modules

Extensions and complementary models

  • Blackwell’s comparison of experiments
  • Cheap talk without commitment

R Readings

A starter reading list

Core papers introduce the main mathematical objects. Further readings develop alternative formulations, limits, and recent directions.

Module 01

Feedback and social objectives

Core

Deep Reinforcement Learning from Human Preferences ↗

Christiano et al. 2017

The modern preference-learning pipeline in its clearest early form.

Core

Training Language Models to Follow Instructions with Human Feedback ↗

Ouyang et al. 2022

A large-scale account of supervised instruction tuning and RLHF.

Further

Three Brief Proofs of Arrow’s Impossibility Theorem ↗

Geanakoplos 2005

Compact routes into the central impossibility result for aggregation.

Further

Cardinal Welfare, Individualistic Ethics, and Interpersonal Comparisons of Utility ↗

Harsanyi 1955

A canonical route from individual utility to utilitarian aggregation.

Further

Axioms for AI Alignment from Human Feedback ↗

Ge et al. 2024

Studies reward learning directly through the axioms of social choice.

Module 02

Preference optimization

Core

Direct Preference Optimization ↗

Rafailov et al. 2023

Recasts a regularized RLHF objective as a direct classification loss.

Core

A Minimaximalist Approach to Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback ↗

Swamy et al. 2024

Uses game-theoretic optimization to avoid assuming a scalar reward.

Further

Nash Learning from Human Feedback ↗

Munos et al. 2023

Targets a regularized Nash equilibrium of a learned preference model.

Further

Distributional Preference Learning ↗

Siththaranjan, Laidlaw & Hadfield-Menell 2024

Models hidden context and disagreement instead of averaging them away.

Further

Distortion of AI Alignment ↗

Gölz, Haghtalab & Yang 2025

Quantifies the welfare loss inherent in comparison-only elicitation.

Module 03

Prediction and agreement

Core

The Well-Calibrated Bayesian ↗

Dawid 1982

A foundational treatment of calibration and probabilistic forecasts.

Core

Omnipredictors ↗

Gopalan et al. 2022

One predictor can support near-optimal decisions for many losses.

Further

An Analog of the Minimax Theorem for Vector Payoffs ↗

Blackwell 1956

The approachability theorem behind many calibration guarantees.

Further

Agreeing to Disagree ↗

Aumann 1976

The classic common-knowledge argument for agreement among Bayesians.

Further

Tractable Agreement Protocols ↗

Collina et al. 2025

Computationally efficient protocols for human–AI agreement.

Module 04

Oversight and assistance

Core

AI Safety via Debate ↗

Irving, Christiano & Amodei 2018

Proposes adversarial argument as a route to scalable oversight.

Core

Bayesian Persuasion ↗

Kamenica & Gentzkow 2011

The foundational model of strategic information design with commitment.

Further

Scalable AI Safety via Doubly-Efficient Debate ↗

Brown-Cohen, Irving & Piliouras 2024

Formalizes debate protocols efficient for both judge and debaters.

Further

Cooperative Inverse Reinforcement Learning ↗

Hadfield-Menell et al. 2016

Treats assistance as a cooperative game with uncertain human rewards.

Further

The Off-Switch Game ↗

Hadfield-Menell et al. 2017

Shows how uncertainty over objectives can create incentives for deference.

Module 05

Institutions and competition

Core

Emergent Alignment via Competition ↗

Collina et al. 2025

Studies when strategic competition among misaligned systems can benefit users.

Further

Strategic Information Transmission ↗

Crawford & Sobel 1982

The canonical model of informative cheap talk without commitment.
